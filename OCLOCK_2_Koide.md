## **1\. Introduction**

### **1.1 The Koide Formula**

In 1981, Yoshio Koide discovered an empirical relation among the charged lepton masses:

**(√mₑ \+ √mμ \+ √mτ)² / (mₑ \+ mμ \+ mτ) \= 2/3**

This relation holds with remarkable precision — better than 0.1% using current measured values. The formula can be parameterized in trigonometric form:

**√mₖ \= m₀\[1 \+ √2 · cos(δ \+ 2πk/3)\], k \= 0, 1, 2**

where m₀ \= (√mₑ \+ √mμ \+ √mτ)/3 sets the overall scale, and the phase δ determines the relative masses. From experimental values, δ ≈ 12.73°.

The origin of this specific phase has remained unexplained for over forty years.

## **5\. Discussion**

### **5.1 Geometric Interpretation**

The 144-clock provides a natural geometric home for the Koide formula. The number 144 is divisible by 3, allowing clean 120° phase separation. The angular step 2.5° is fine enough to accommodate the 1/11 tick fractional offset while remaining discrete.

### **5.2 Comparison to Prior Approaches**

Previous attempts to explain the Koide phase — mass matrix textures, finite group symmetries (A₄, S₄), anomaly cancellation — all leave δ as a free parameter. Our approach derives δ from clock arithmetic without fitting it to lepton masses.

### **5.3 Limitations**

1. **Absolute scale:** The derivation gives the phase δ but not m₀. The electron mass must be taken as input. This remains an open problem.  
2. **Electron sensitivity:** The electron's near-cancellation (denominator ≈ 0.039) makes mass predictions sensitive to small errors in δ. Even our 0.00003° discrepancy gets amplified.  
3. **Prime selection:** Why primes 5 and 11 specifically control the lepton phase, rather than other primes outside 144, is not derived from first principles. It is a pattern, not yet a derivation.  
4. **Neutrino masses:** The Koide formula applies to charged leptons. The neutrino sector may involve different primes — this connection remains unexplored.

### **5.4 Open Questions**

1. Can m₀ be derived from the electroweak scale or Planck scale?  
2. Does a similar prime-based phase exist for quarks?  
3. Can the Koide formula be extended to the neutrino sector?  
4. Why is the clock size uniquely 144 for this structure?

The derivation uses primes 5 and 11\. Prime 5 appears independently as the base action prime (S₀ \= 2π \+ 5/144). Prime 11 appears as the lepton correction. Both are the smallest primes greater than 3 that do not divide 144, consistent with a pattern of prime assignments across the framework.

The expression δ \= (5 \+ 1/11) × 2.5° should be understood as a compact empirical observation relating the Koide phase to the clock's prime structure, not a first-principles derivation.

The numerical agreement is striking. Whether it reflects deep structure or a numerical coincidence enabled by the richness of mod-144 arithmetic is an open question this paper cannot resolve — but raises clearly.
